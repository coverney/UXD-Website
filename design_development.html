Overall Process

For the design development phase, we focused on translating our user’s and persona’s needs into effective design decisions.

What is the design?

Our current design is focused on connecting empty nester parents to families applying to colleges for the first time. Our users can create profiles and then get matched to empty nesters or applicants. From there, empty nester parents can give college application advice through forums, blog posts, notifications, and essay editing platforms. We want to create a friendly environment where our users can establish new connections centered around the college application process. Even after the application is completed, matched users are encouraged to keep each other updated on college admission results.

How is this design responsive to user needs?

Considering our target user being closely related to our “Relaxed Russels,” and “Busy Bailey” personas, we developed both sides of our interface, the student-parent side and the empty nester side, with the people who desire a simpler interface that thoroughly conveys the messages and ideas desired while being concise and easy to follow. The needs of those who felt constrained on time along with those who were in need of immediate assistance where primarily taken into account. In designing our site, we took into account how our users felt on tangibility vs. emotional outcome of this produce, while also keeping into account their commitment levels. For one persona, “Overly Available Olivers,” we saw that they served the group of empty nesters who desired emotional connections as an outcome with high commitment levels while another persona, “Busy Baileys,” had low commitment level, but still desired something tangible and emotional. Because of these observations, we developed our site to include a matching system that is quick and simple, in order to match the low commitment of “Busy Baileys” while also keeping in account interests and skills for this matching systems. This allows one to develop an emotional connection to the people they help while still being helpful to students going through the College Application Process.

How well does it meet user needs? In what ways does it fall short?

From our user test with a current empty nester parent, we learned that our service needs to be presented in a more easy going light, rather than a business transaction. This is because the purpose for the empty nester is to provide their experience, along with helping young students similar to their kids. In order to make this interaction more “personal” our user suggested matches to “add a note” or a personal message to their perspective matches. We also learned what our user did like about the user experience. We learned that the user really enjoys the setup separated by column because it resembles that of an email inbox and they know how to navigate that. Furthermore, we saw from testing that this type of user enjoys images and icons instead of words. Lastly, the empty nester user wants to match with students/families interested in the same school their child attends or type of school, because it will lead to a more rich relationship.

Ideating, Listening, & Iterating

Starting the design development phase, we had a well-rounded understanding of our users through 12 interviews, which we used to create 4 personas. For each persona, we considered their values. Our goal was to map values to features that our users would want. From persona values, we brainstormed features that directly connected with user values: We also considered user safety: Scenario brainstorming by persona: Mapping features to scenarios: From there, we decided to create paper prototypes for the user profile and user matching aspects of our project. The paper prototypes were ideal for iterating on our ideas and finding aspects of our design that disagreed with user values.

User Profile

We started designing the user profile by creating a bunch of concept sketches, a few of which are shown below. We found many commonalities among our sketches in terms of what sort of information we should include in the user profile. We all agreed that the following user attributes were necessary to collect:

  • Name
  • Pronoun
  • Empty Nester or Applicant Status
  • Profile picture
  • Languages
  • Engagement Level
  • Types of schools user is interested in or knows about
  • Specific things user needs help in or can help with
Additionally, we thought of some nice-to-have features that could be used to generate better matches:
  • Hobbies
  • Goals relevant to the matching service
  • Location(used to generate a map of rough location of where the user is located
Considering user safety, we decided to keep the user profiles private with a separate, more general baseball-card like public profile that would be used in the matching process. We integrated our concept sketches into the user profile template shown below. While we did not focus on user profiles in our usability tests, we received some relevant feedback, such as having filled in circles instead of sliding bars to represent engagement level, encouraging profile pictures over avatars, and making sure to thoroughly walk through the profile creation process step by step. We plan to take inspiration from the dating app, Hinge’s onboarding process, in which we ask for user information in an interactive and conversational manner.
