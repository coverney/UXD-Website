Design Development Phase

For the design development phase, we focused on translating our user’s and persona’s needs into effective design decisions.

What is the design?

Our current design is focused on connecting empty nester parents to families applying to colleges for the first time. Our users can create profiles and then get matched to empty nesters or applicants. From there, empty nester parents can give college application advice through forums, blog posts, notifications, and essay editing platforms. We want to create a friendly environment where our users can establish new connections centered around the college application process. Even after the application is completed, matched users are encouraged to keep each other updated on college admission results.

How is this design responsive to user needs?

As our project involved two different user groups, we designed with both empty nesters and families applying to colleges in mind. Common to all of our users was the need for a simple, concise interface that is easy to navigate. However, it is particularly relevant to the personas “Busy Baileys” and “Relaxed Russels,” both of which have a limited time commitment and prefered minimalism over more features. It also makes sense for those who are in need of immediate application assistance. In designing our site, we also took into account how the tradeoff between our users’ feelings on tangibility vs. emotional outcome of this product. Our persona “Overly Available Olivers” describes empty nesters who value emotional connections and high commitment levels while “Busy Baileys” have relatively low commitment levels and desire tangible results over emotional connection. Because of these observations, we developed our site to include a matching system that is simple and quick. This matches with the lower commitment of our “Busy Baileys” persona while keeping in mind more holistic aspects such as interests and skills that the emotionally invested “Olivers” value. This balanced the emotional connection between our users while still making sure the students get the help they need with the college application process.





Ideation Process

Starting the design development phase, we had a well-rounded understanding of our users through 12 interviews, which we used to create 4 personas. For each persona, we considered their values. Our goal was to map values to features that our users would want. From the persona values, we brainstormed situations our users find themselves in and translated them into features that directly connected with their values. For example, “Overly-Available Olivers” are more prone to a sit-down environment when working so a platform for them could include features like autosave, break reminders, or a timer. Meanwhile, “Busy Baileys” has a time constraint and would benefit more from features such as a mobile platform, notifications with quick reply features, read receipts, or resource suggestions that they can link for their student.

We also did some generic feature brainstorming that we grouped into 5 categories: user-website interaction, user-mentor interaction, time management, connection to resources, and user community.

However, for this phase we chose to focus on the matching and user profile aspect of our website, so these features are not yet tested with our users. User safety was a constant concern for our design. We wanted to ensure a helpful and meaningful interaction between our users but had to design around unsafe situations such as inappropriate messages, users purposefully giving bad advice, implicit discrmination when matching, and college essay plagiarism. A few features we brainstormed relating to user safety include being able to end connections by choice and fact-checking advice.

Finally, we decided to create paper prototypes for two very important aspects of our project: the user profile and user matching. The paper prototypes were ideal for iterating on our ideas and finding aspects of our designs that disagreed with user values.

User Profile

We started designing the user profile by creating concept sketches, a few of which are shown below. We found many commonalities among our sketches in terms of what sort of information should be included in the user profile. We all agreed that the following user attributes were necessary to collect:

  • Name
  • Pronoun
  • Empty Nester or Applicant Status
  • Profile picture
  • Languages
  • Engagement Level
  • Types of schools user is interested in or knows about
  • Specific things user needs help in or can help with

Considering user safety, we decided to keep the user profiles private with a separate, more general baseball-card like public profile that would be used in the matching process. While we did not focus on user profiles in our usability tests, we received some relevant feedback, such as having filled in circles instead of sliding bars to represent engagement level, encouraging profile pictures over avatars, and making sure to thoroughly walk through the profile creation process step by step. We plan to take inspiration from the dating app, Hinge’s onboarding process, in which we ask for user information in an interactive and conversational manner.

User Matching

We wanted to invest time to make sure we designed a user-matching interface that supports our goal of connecting empty nesters and college applicant families to form meaningful relationships. There were a few different ways we could match our users including suggesting matches based on user profiles, matching people based on tasks, and having college applicant families reach out to empty nesters that they are interested in working with. Using the concept sketches, we conducted four users tests, one with an empty nester and three with applying families.

From our user test with a current empty nester parent, we learned that our service needs to be presented in a more easy going light, rather than a business transaction. This is because the purpose for the empty nester is to share their experiences, along with helping young students similar to their kids. To make this interaction more “personal”, our user suggested matches to “add a note” or a personal message to their perspective matches. We learned that our user really enjoys an interface separated by columns because it resembles that of an email inbox, and they know how to navigate that. Furthermore, we saw from testing that this type of user enjoys images and icons instead of words. Lastly, the empty nester user wants to match with students/families interested in the same school or type of school their child attends, because they believe it will lead to a more rich relationship.

From the applying family tests, there were a wide range of ideas but the three main categories were general comments, comments on the task-matching format, and comments on the hero card format.

Overall, the test users confirmed the idea that simplistic summaries were helpful, but there was a conflict between the applying-families’ and empty nesters’ perspectives about what the summaries should include. The students believed that the summaries should include more application-relevant information, especially because there was an inherent distrust of the match score or “best match” label seen in two of the interviews that points to a need for transparency in the matching process. However, the empty nester enjoyed being able to share and match on outside interests such as hobbies that encouraged emotional connections. This discrepancy is most likely due to the difference in the desired emotional vs tangible results and will have to be resolved in further iterations of our prototypes.

Besides this, there was also concern about the engagement level being shown to other users so in future iterations it should be used for matching only as a back-end algorithm. However, more match options are not needed as the two designs lent themselves to a nice balance of choice and simplicity. Specific comments about the preference for one format over the other tended towards the hero card idea. The test users liked the idea of a single, long-term contact to guide them both for the additional security and the personal connection. Otherwise, the test user mentioned that it would feel too business-like.

As for task-based matching comments, there was a consensus that the impersonal and results-focused concept would be best for families that already have some knowledge about the application process and/or those who would be comfortable being the driving brain behind the application process. This may not work very well for our persona “Relaxed Russels,” but could benefit “Busy Baileys.” There is also not enough emotional involvement for “Overly-Available Olivers.”

The hero card idea was generally more positively received because it gives the interface more personality and uses more icons/images than words. The test users liked the idea of one main contact as it was more personal and better for students who weren’t knowledgeable about the application process. However, the current format has a less intuitive interaction method in that it looks more suited to swiping or dragging than scrolling and clicking. One user mentioned that the large amount of space each card took up was good for quickly understanding potential matches but if the match is for a long-time they would be willing to invest even more time to learn about the match. Lastly, if the match is one on one, the empty nester or third party should initiate the conversation in order set an interaction expectation.
